That’s 32 of 60 cards, so let’s fill in the other 28 cards of this blue-red [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) deck, shall we?

The card I’m building around this week is [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler). The Chronicler has three abilities, two of which are tied together. Let’s take a look at the card, and see which cards would be best used in conjunction with this rare.

**Ability #1:**[Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler)’s power and toughness are equal to the number of cards in your hand.

**Building Goal #1:** Does this ability look familiar? Word for word (excepting the name of the creature, for you nitpickers out there), this is the same ability as [Maro](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/254/maro)’s. If [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) had no other text, it would be a [Maro](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/254/maro) in a different color, at one more mana. What does [Maro](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/254/maro) need to work? Cards. Lots of cards in hand!

The first goal of building an [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) deck, therefore, is to find a way to keep your hand stocked with cards. This can be accomplished through many ways.

You can play a lot of cantrips – cards which allow you to draw a card when cast – such as [Electrolyze](https://scryfall.com/card/ima/198/electrolyze) and [Repeal](https://scryfall.com/card/clu/94/repeal). This way, you can both affect the board, and keep your hand size up at the same time.

You can play spells specifically designed to draw cards, such as [Tidings](https://scryfall.com/card/c18/108/tidings) or [Dimir Guildmage](https://scryfall.com/card/clu/186/dimir-guildmage). These cards will keep your hand replenished, and allow your [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) to be huge.

You can play spells which can be returned to your hand after being cast. This includes buyback spells such as [Whispers of the Muse](https://scryfall.com/card/wc98/rb103/whispers-of-the-muse), or cards which return from the graveyard, such as [Shard Phoenix](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/217/shard-phoenix) (which, happily, is already a carry-over from last week!)

You can play spells which affect multiple targets at once, to minimize the number of spells you need to play before [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) hits the board. This includes playing cards like [Pyroclasm](https://scryfall.com/card/dsk/149/pyroclasm) instead of [Volcanic Hammer](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/226/volcanic-hammer), or [Boom // Bust](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/156/boom-bust) (for Bust) instead of [Stone Rain](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/221/stone-rain).

**Ability #2:** Suspend X – {X}{3}{U}. X can’t be 0. Whenever a time counter is removed from [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) while it’s removed from the game, draw a card.

**Building Goal #2:** The minimum suspend cost of [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) is therefore five mana – one blue, three generic, and an “X” value greater than zero. This allows you to use [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) in one of two ways – you can play him as a Nantuko Shaman, or as an uncounterable [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) that eventually comes into play as a large creature.

If you’re playing [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) as a Nantuko Shaman, you can consider the following: [Maro](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/254/maro) costs {2}{G}{G} to cast. If you suspend [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) at five mana, you get a cantrip [Maro](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/254/maro) with haste (when creatures unsuspend, they enter play with haste). The two abilities – drawing a card when a time counter is removed, and having power and toughness equal to the number of cards in your hand – are synergistic.

If you’re playing [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) as a [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena), then you want a ton of mana. The longer [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) stays out of play, the more turns you’ll draw an extra card each upkeep. In order to power this strategy, you’re going to need to play a lot of lands, mana acceleration, and be able to drag the game out past the first few turns. The advantage you have here, over playing [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena), is that you cannot counter the suspend ability itself. Suspending a card is an uncounterable ability. You *can* counter the creature once it unsuspends – that is when it becomes a spell that can be countered. You can even counter the counter-removing or card-drawing triggers with something like Trickbind. However, paying a blue and four colorless to remove [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) from the game with one time counter cannot be stopped with a Counterspell. Unlike [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena), a suspended [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) can’t be killed by [Krosan Grip](https://scryfall.com/card/c21/198/krosan-grip) or [Disenchant](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/572/disenchant), can’t be bounced by [Boomerang](https://scryfall.com/card/5ed/75/boomerang), and doesn’t ping you for a point of life each upkeep.

[![Maro](https://api.scryfall.com/cards/9ed/254?format=image&version=normal)](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/254/maro)

For the first build of the deck, I wanted to focus on the former – using [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) as a hasty, cantrip [Maro](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/254/maro). This means that I could concentrate more on card drawing, and less on mana acceleration. To this effect, here’s what I put into the deck:

Add: 4 [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler)

A no-brainer, considering this is the guy we’re building the deck around!

Add: 4 [Electrolyze](https://scryfall.com/card/ima/198/electrolyze)

This fits under the cantrip category, and it can kill two one-toughness creatures, or one two-toughness creature.

Add: 4 [Compulsive Research](https://scryfall.com/card/tdc/147/compulsive-research)

This is the best pure card-drawing spell available to blue in Standard right now, and competes with [Harmonize](https://scryfall.com/card/msc/175/harmonize) for the title of best one-shot card drawing spell in Standard, amongst all colors. In a deck that wants to have cards in hand, this is a shoo-in.

Add: 3 [Wheel of Fate](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/198/wheel-of-fate) and 4 [Timecrafting](https://scryfall.com/card/plc/109/timecrafting)

These were the last additions to the deck. [Wheel of Fate](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/198/wheel-of-fate) allows me to draw seven cards at once, but on a four-turn clock. If I don’t have a lot of cards in hand, this is a quick way to get [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) into full form using only one spell. To better facilitate quick [Wheel of Fate](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/198/wheel-of-fate)s, I decided to try out [Timecrafting](https://scryfall.com/card/plc/109/timecrafting). [Timecrafting](https://scryfall.com/card/plc/109/timecrafting) servers a dual purpose – I can speed up a [Wheel of Fate](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/198/wheel-of-fate) at the end of an opponent’s turn, so I can untap with a full hand, or I can speed [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) into play, and draw a lot of cards in the process. For instance, if [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) is suspended with three counters, and I hit it with [Timecrafting](https://scryfall.com/card/plc/109/timecrafting) for three (removing the counters), I draw three cards. Drawing cards from counters being removed from [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) is not contingent on having them be removed via your upkeep – if you remove them in any way, you draw cards from the Chronicler.

Add: 9 [Island](https://scryfall.com/card/msh/289/island)s

To be honest, this deck is still more red-heavy than blue-heavy, and this is a deck that wants to run *Ravnica* bouncelands ([Izzet Boilerworks](https://scryfall.com/card/otc/302/izzet-boilerworks) in particular) instead of [Terramorphic Expanse](https://scryfall.com/card/msc/273/terramorphic-expanse) – returning a land to your hand to fuel [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler) while advancing your mana count is better than searching a land out of your deck for this build. This change will be made after the first update for the deck, unless [Terramorphic Expanse](https://scryfall.com/card/msc/273/terramorphic-expanse) proves to be an all-star.

[![Wheel of Fate](https://api.scryfall.com/cards/tsr/198?format=image&version=normal)](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/198/wheel-of-fate)

**Rules Primer #1:** Does [Wheel of Fate](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/198/wheel-of-fate) kill [Aeon Chronicler](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/51/aeon-chronicler)? The answer is no! [Wheel of Fate](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/198/wheel-of-fate) reads: “Each player discards his or her hand, then draws seven cards.” Although there is a time during the resolution of [Wheel of Fate](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/198/wheel-of-fate) at which you have no cards in hand, state-based effects (the rules which check to see if zero-toughness creatures die) are not checked during the resolution of a spell or effect. Therefore, since discarding your hand and drawing seven cards are all part of the same spell, there is no time at which the rules will see that you have zero cards in hand for your Chronicler, making him safe from death by Wheel!

Memo to myself – this deck needs bounce. I need to be able to remove threats from the board to buy time to set up my game. [Repeal](https://scryfall.com/card/clu/94/repeal) is a natural add in this deck, as it’s a cantrip. The other bounce spell I decide to run is [Riftwing Cloudskate](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/82/riftwing-cloudskate) – it works well with [Timecrafting](https://scryfall.com/card/plc/109/timecrafting) and [Rough // Tumble](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/186/rough-tumble), and it gives me another offensive threat for the deck.

To make room for these cards, I gank out [Avalanche Riders](https://scryfall.com/card/tsb/55/avalanche-riders) (easily worth as much, monetarily, as the rest of the deck put together!) and [Cloudstone Curio](https://scryfall.com/card/rvr/255/cloudstone-curio). I mainly kill the Riders not out of budgetary concerns (the deck is well under 30 tickets), but because players really don’t want to play against land destruction. Last week, there was some concern about my ability to destroy four to six lands a turn. While I personally have no problem with a splash of land destruction in a deck (after all, how else do you deal with threats like [Academy Ruins](https://scryfall.com/card/2xm/309/academy-ruins), Urborg, or Tomb of Urami?), I will try to do my best to avoid it unless it’s an integral part of the deck. Last week, [Avalanche Riders](https://scryfall.com/card/tsb/55/avalanche-riders) worked well with [Cloudstone Curio](https://scryfall.com/card/rvr/255/cloudstone-curio) and [Wild Pair](https://scryfall.com/card/cn2/196/wild-pair). This week, it’s out of theme, and therefore gone – along with [Cloudstone Curio](https://scryfall.com/card/rvr/255/cloudstone-curio) itself.

Time for more changes. I realize that I’ve had no problem winning games against slower, control-oriented decks, as I can outdraw them – and in the control on control match, he who draws more cards (which translate into options) wins. My weakness is against weenie decks. To shore up these deficits in my deck, I change up some cards.

First, I add in 3 [Pyroclasm](https://scryfall.com/card/dsk/149/pyroclasm). This gives me a total of 6 spells that can wipe out 1 and 2 toughness creatures. Plus, the more [Rough // Tumble](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/186/rough-tumble)/[Pyroclasm](https://scryfall.com/card/dsk/149/pyroclasm) I have, the more of a chance I’ll draw two in a game and be able to take out larger creatures en-masse.

In addition, I want some mana acceleration in the deck. I’ve had several games where I’m stuck playing one spell a turn, with several options in hand – or stuck with a [Shard Phoenix](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/217/shard-phoenix) in hand and one mana short of wiping out my opponent’s board. Good ol’ standby [Izzet Signet](https://scryfall.com/card/tdc/320/izzet-signet) fits the bill here.

To make room for these spells, I remove one [Mountain](https://scryfall.com/card/msc/858/mountain) (which four [Izzet Signet](https://scryfall.com/card/tdc/320/izzet-signet)s should more-than-compensate for), the [Stingscourger](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/192/stingscourger) and [Blood Knight](https://scryfall.com/card/plc/115/blood-knight), and the four [Electrolyze](https://scryfall.com/card/ima/198/electrolyze). [Electrolyze](https://scryfall.com/card/ima/198/electrolyze) seemed good in theory, but it never really killed any creatures worth noting. It can’t kill [Silhana Ledgewalker](https://scryfall.com/card/otc/206/silhana-ledgewalker), and it’s slow against other guys. I’d rather take out [Birds of Paradise](https://scryfall.com/card/msc/170/birds-of-paradise) and [Silhana Ledgewalker](https://scryfall.com/card/otc/206/silhana-ledgewalker) with [Pyroclasm](https://scryfall.com/card/dsk/149/pyroclasm) on turn two than kill only Birds on turn three.
