# Building On A Budget - Dark Confinement

**Author:** Nate Heiss  
**Date:** April 21, 2003  
**Original URL (Wayback Machine):** <https://web.archive.org/web/20090601202842/https://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=magic/magiconline/articlenh042103>

---

[Last week](https://web.archive.org/web/20090604155218/https://www.wizards.com/Magic/TCG/Article.aspx?x=magic/magiconline/articlenh041403) I said that in order to make a less expensive deck, powerful cards can be substituted for two things: speed and synergistic combinations of less powerful cards. There is one more way to make a good deck that is not so pricey--one of my favorite ways: Go Rogue.

Call of the Herd

Card prices are a factor of how many copies of a card are used in a deck and how popular that deck is. This is why looking at card prices is important for predicting the metagame. In fact, any good storeowner should know roughly what makes up the metagame so he can appropriately price the cards. This is why, if Upheavals and Call of the Herd sell for nearly 20 tickets each, Tog and Red-Green are probably popular. It's simple supply and demand.

However, Rogue decks typically don't use cards that many people play or are based on a strategy that isn't popular. Instead, Rogue decks try to broadside the format in unusual ways with surprising cards. With luck, no one will be prepared for a Rogue deck and it can run away winning game after game. Eventually, when enough people start playing a successful Rogue deck, it becomes a part of the standard metagame and becomes a target like the rest.

I particularly like Rogue decks, not only because they are usually less pricy to build, but because my opponents often have no idea how to play against them. You've probably practiced against Tog, Red-Green, White-Green, Blue-Green, and Slide and know how to play your deck against any of these. Imagine this scenario:

What if your opponent started playing swamps and islands and then started using Compulsion? They're playing Tog, right?

But wait--did your opponent just Compulsion away Ghastly Remains? What's going on? He doesn't seem to be putting up much of a fight--he just keeps drawing cards and taking damage. What is that card he just played? Solitary Confinement? What does that do? Oh...now how do I win?

I cannot even begin to tell you how it feels when someone has to reach over, pick up your card, and read it. Online this works a little differently--usually there will be a long pause after your spell resolves. Take pride in the fact that your opponent is probably looking at the screen wondering how any respectable player could do this. How lucky for you!

Here is one of many Rogue decks that can be built on a low ticket count but still pack a wallop:

##### How The Deck Works

Solitary Confinement

Simply put, you want to keep Solitary Confinement around for an indefinite amount of time while still being able to do a little bit of other stuff, eventually winning the game with Millstone. Your goal is to have Compulsion and Solitary Confinement with a Ghastly Remains. You need three black mana, one blue mana, and one other available. This is pretty much the absolute minimum to get lockdown. You could run it earlier, but it would be on a wing and a prayer.

Smother and Aether Burst will slow down your opponent a little while you go through your deck with Compulsion, Obsessive Search, and Concentrate. If you do not have a Ghastly Remains, Compulsion, or Solitary Confinement in your opening hand, seriously consider a mulligan. It's just too hard to get all three if you do not already start with one. If you start with Compulsion, then it is rather easy.

The strength of this deck is that it plays well against any aggressive creature- or burn-based deck, considering most decks have no answer to a Solitary Confinement soft-lock. However, the deck has trouble with control decks, like Tog, because most have some way to deal with Confinement or Millstone and pack much more counter-magic than you. In short, if the Tog player resolves a 19-ticket Upheaval, you can say goodnight. If you Millstone the Upheavals away, they can kiss their dreams goodbye.

##### Playing The Deck

- Ghastly Remains If you can, discard an unimportant card to Solitary Confinement and wait until your opponent's end step before using Compulsion to put Ghastly Remains in your graveyard. This helps protect you against sorcery-speed graveyard removal effects (like Morningtide) and will let you Smother a newly-cast Withered Wretch before it eats your game plan.
- It's sometimes worth the five mana to bring Ghastly Remains back and Compulsion it away.
- When you achieve the lock, you will often only have one or two cards in your hand and only two or three mana to work with. Take your time and slowly Compulsion your way into an Obsessive Search or Concentrate, letting you accumulate more cards in hand and play out more land. Eventually you'll get double Ghastly Remains going with Compulsion and build your way to the perfect handful of counters and Millstone--if your opponent doesn't concede first, of course.
- Play quickly! You're fighting the clock more than your opponent sometimes. Get used to stacking things on your upkeep in a timely fashion. You may find it necessary to deck your opponent the hard way, which is difficult in ***Magic** Online*.

##### Sideboarding Tips

After sideboarding is when things get tricky. If your opponent is playing beatdown and forgot to put enchantment removal in her sideboard, well, that's Good Game. However, most players will have some sort of enchantment removal, whether it is Ray of Revelation, Naturalize, Disenchant, or Nantuko Vigilante. At this point, the key to the match rests on a race to your counterspells. You not only have to get your lock out, but you also have to stockpile countermagic before your opponent kills your Confinement. Thus, some of your sideboard should be based around protecting it.

Liquify

**Liquify** 

 Crude but effective, Liquify will counter just about any enchantment-removal spell and can be boarded in against Psychatog to help in the counter war.

**Devoted Caretaker** 

Devoted Caretaker is a good bet versus the Green-White and Blue-Green decks unless they are running the Vigilante. It doesn't work against Red-Green though.

**Envelop** 

 More anti-Upheaval cards are always helpful, and Envelop is good versus Mono-Black as well.

**Duress/Persecute**

Duress and Persecute are more anti-Tog cards--you'll at least draw out some counters out with these.

##### Conclusion

If you want to add a little more money to the deck, I would definitely say go for the lands. This deck is dying for City of Brass and another two Flooded Strands, or any combination of pricey multicolor lands. There is another, more expensive way to build the deck using a green build instead of black, with Genesis, Living Wish, Pain-lands, Quiet Speculation, and Moment's Peace. It's a more solid way to do things, but vastly more expensive.
