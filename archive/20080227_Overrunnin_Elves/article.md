# Overrunnin' Elves

**Author:** Ben Bleiweiss  
**Date:** February 27, 2008  
**Original URL (Wayback Machine):** <https://web.archive.org/web/20090601202842/https://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/daily/bb153>

---

Hello everyone, and welcome back to Building on a Budget. Just a couple of weeks until *Morningtide* is released online—and I know that you're as excited about that as I am! In the meanwhile, I was taking a look around my file of criminally underused cards to see which cards would be interesting to build a deck around. That's when I hit upon [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun).

Once upon a time, long long ago, I evolved [the Fun with Fungus theme deck](https://web.archive.org/web/20090603041538/https://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/daily/bb96). One of the additions I made to the deck was [Coat of Arms](https://scryfall.com/card/dds/58/coat-of-arms). [Coat of Arms](https://scryfall.com/card/dds/58/coat-of-arms) gave me a way to leverage large quantities of creatures of one type into huge amounts of damage.

[Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun) and [Coat of Arms](https://scryfall.com/card/dds/58/coat-of-arms) are similar in many ways—both let your large hordes of creature turn into huge creatures that deal massive amount of damage in one turn. In today's online metagame, though, [Coat of Arms](https://scryfall.com/card/dds/58/coat-of-arms) can often be a liability.

For those not familiar with the term metagame, it is a fancy way of saying "the pool of decks that are currently being played a lot in your area." In this case, the area is all of **Magic Online**'s casual room. Everybody and their sister is playing some sort of tribal deck—and this means that [Coat of Arms](https://scryfall.com/card/dds/58/coat-of-arms), which pumps *both* your creatures, and your opponent's creatures, can often be as much of a liability as it is a strength. [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun), on the other hand, only makes your guys bigger.

[![Overrun](https://api.scryfall.com/cards/fdn/230?format=image&version=normal)](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun)

In addition, trample is a huge way to get through enough damage to win. I played a lot of *Tempest* booster draft (in fact, I had my highest Pro Tour finish at Pro Tour–Mainz, which was *Tempest* / *Tempest* / *Tempest* booster draft), and green was universally considered the weakest color in straight-*Tempest* draft. White and black had a lot of efficient evasion creatures because of shadow, red had the best common in the set (Rolling Thunder) and a lot of burn spells, and blue had a ridiculous number of control cards, including [Counterspell](https://scryfall.com/card/dsc/114/counterspell) and Capsize. This left green as the clunky color that could neither control the game nor kill people quickly with weenies.

The one strength green had was, ironically, that green was usually avoided because it was so weak. This meant that at any given table, if only one or two people were concentrating on building a primarily green deck, they might end up with a really strong deck. If six people are drafting black, and only two people drafting green, the green (split two ways) is going to be stronger than the black (split six ways) even if black is a much stronger color than green.

What's all this have to do with Building on a Budget? Hold on, I'm getting to it! So green was generally the worst of the five colors in straight *Tempest* draft, but green had one card which everyone feared: [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun). [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun) was considered one of the most unfair cards printed for Draft—and this was even more the case once *Odyssey* block came around, [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun) was reprinted, and green was probably the *best* color in that set for Limited play!

Even to this day, I know how to do [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun) math. 3(X) + Y – Z = Dead, where X is the number of creatures you control, Y is the combined power of those creatures, and Z is the combined toughness of your opponent's creatures. I've played many a game where my opponent (or I) went from 20 life to 0 life in a single swing due to [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun). Giving all your creatures trample is the key to making this work—otherwise, your opponent can just chump block and go their merry way.

So if I were to build a deck around [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun), I'd need to keep two facts in mind:

1. The more creatures I have in play, the more extra damage I get to do with [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun) (which is a Giant Growth with trample for every creature I control).
2. The majority of my deck, if not the entirety of my deck, has to be green, since [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun) has a very strict color requirement ({G}{G}{G})

Thankfully, the Elves of Standard have many creature token generators. This meant that I could easily fuse the above two goals into one deck, and not hurt for the color requirements. This deck was going to be Standard using **Magic Online**, meaning that *Morningtide* is not yet legal. Here's the list I came up with for the deck (with ticket costs per card in parenthesis):

I wanted to have early acceleration in order to drop my curve ahead of the one land / one turn schedule—if I could [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun) on turn five with five creatures in play, I'd have a minimum of 20 points of trample damage coming across on the board. [Boreal Druid](https://scryfall.com/card/csp/105/boreal-druid) and [Llanowar Elves](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/227/llanowar-elves) were the two creatures I chose for this task. I combined them with [Scryb Ranger](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/227/scryb-ranger), so say, if I had four lands and a [Llanowar Elves](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/227/llanowar-elves) on the board on turn four, I could both tap my Elf for mana and then attack with it for four.

I also wanted several ways to generate large quantities of token creatures, to cheat on the creature count. Remember—if I have [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun), every creature I have is going to swing for a minimum of four damage, so it doesn't matter if they are 1/1 tokens or 4/4 tokens—quantity is definitely desired over quantity. [Imperious Perfect](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/719/imperious-perfect), the ubiquitous elf generator and lord, was first on the list—he works well with my other mana creatures and other token generators, which are sure to be elves as well. I also wanted [Gilt-Leaf Ambush](https://scryfall.com/card/lrw/214/gilt-leaf-ambush) (which could make two elves in one turn), [Lys Alana Huntmaster](https://scryfall.com/card/khc/67/lys-alana-huntmaster) (which benefits from every other elf cast), [Wren's Run Packmaster](https://scryfall.com/card/cma/171/wrens-run-packmaster) (to generate wolves), and [Thelonite Hermit](https://scryfall.com/card/mkc/188/thelonite-hermit).

I really loved the Hermit in my Saproling deck, and I decided to use him again here. Although this deck isn't Fungus intensive by any means, [Thelonite Hermit](https://scryfall.com/card/mkc/188/thelonite-hermit) is a 1/1 that, effectively, puts four 2/2 creatures into play. So:

3X + Y – Z = Dead

3 x (5 creatures) + 9 (power) – Z (toughness of my opponent's creatures) = 24 (damage being dealt with just two cards).

That's not a bad deal.

I also rounded out the deck with [Harmonize](https://scryfall.com/card/msc/175/harmonize) (to fill my hand after dropping a lot of creatures), [Yavimaya Dryad](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/247/yavimaya-dryad) (to jump from one to three to five—[Llanowar Elves](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/227/llanowar-elves) to [Yavimaya Dryad](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/247/yavimaya-dryad) to five mana on the board), and [Immaculate Magistrate](https://scryfall.com/card/cmr/234/immaculate-magistrate)—which theoretically would give my other Elves huge bodies for just the cost of tapping it.

The good news is that [Overrun](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/230/overrun) is fannnnntastic, and I'm able to turn games around in a single turn from a certain loss into a crushing win. The bad news is that I realllllly could use a way to kill smaller creatures, and green is pretty short on creature removal spells, no?

[![Moonglove Extract](https://api.scryfall.com/cards/ima/222?format=image&version=normal)](https://scryfall.com/card/ima/222/moonglove-extract)

I take a look through green and artifact spells, and I eventually start taking a look at [Moonglove Extract](https://scryfall.com/card/ima/222/moonglove-extract). Now I know what you're thinking—"[Moonglove Extract](https://scryfall.com/card/ima/222/moonglove-extract), Ben? Are you serious? It's a three-mana [Seal of Fire](https://scryfall.com/card/jvc/50/seal-of-fire)—it's horrible!"

Well, it's not the best compared to [Seal of Fire](https://scryfall.com/card/jvc/50/seal-of-fire), I'll give you that. It's not horrible, though, if you're on a budget and you're playing green. Back in the day, White and Black Weenie decks used to run a card from *Fallen Empires* called Aeolipile (look it up!). It was a two-mana artifact that cost one mana to use, and it sacrificed to deal 2 damage to target creature or player—in short, a rare version of [Moonglove Extract](https://scryfall.com/card/ima/222/moonglove-extract) that switched one mana of mana cost for one mana of activation cost.

I had two choices when decided if I wanted [Moonglove Extract](https://scryfall.com/card/ima/222/moonglove-extract) in my deck—did I want to keep the deck mono-green, or did I want to splash a color? In the end, I would rather give up a little *efficiency* to gain a lot of *consistency.* With the amount of green cost for my cards, I wanted to make sure I got all the green mana I needed every game, without the possibility of color screwing myself. What I needed was an early-game way to kill a creature, and [Moonglove Extract](https://scryfall.com/card/ima/222/moonglove-extract) was just that card—it costs two mana more than [Seal of Fire](https://scryfall.com/card/jvc/50/seal-of-fire), but it is colorless, usable in my mono-green deck, and fills my need just fine. Why would I disrupt the entire mana base of my deck just to splash four copies of one card ([Incinerate](https://scryfall.com/card/jvc/51/incinerate)? [Shock](https://scryfall.com/card/msc/809/shock)?) that, in the context of what I needed, was only marginally better than [Moonglove Extract](https://scryfall.com/card/ima/222/moonglove-extract)?

[Dryad Arbor](https://scryfall.com/card/dsc/273/dryad-arbor) dies...a lot! I hated being effectively hit by [Stone Rain](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/221/stone-rain) every time someone killed [Dryad Arbor](https://scryfall.com/card/dsc/273/dryad-arbor), so I think that it needs to go—but what to replace it with? [Utopia Sprawl](https://scryfall.com/card/rvr/159/utopia-sprawl) on [Dryad Arbor](https://scryfall.com/card/dsc/273/dryad-arbor) is cool once you start get [Intruder Alarm](https://scryfall.com/card/8ed/86/intruder-alarm) out, though.

[Merfolk Looter](https://scryfall.com/card/afc/86/merfolk-looter) / [Thought Courier](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/104/thought-courier) is great! It allowed me to dig for the cards I needed when I needed them, and they were definitely a strong addition to the deck. In fact, it's been suggested that the Elves leave the deck in favor of Merfolk once *Morningtide* is legal. While I don't think the Elves would go entirely, a combination of Stonybrook Schoolmaster and Drowner of Secrets would also go infinite with [Intruder Alarm](https://scryfall.com/card/8ed/86/intruder-alarm).

I don't really have any sideboard against [Mind's Desire](https://scryfall.com/card/c21/123/minds-desire), or an aggro deck! In all three matches I played, my sideboard was completely useless. I need to find cards that can help me disrupt [Mind's Desire](https://scryfall.com/card/c21/123/minds-desire) decks—they are going to be played all over the place now that the deck finished extremely well at [Grand Prix–Vancouver](https://web.archive.org/web/20090603041538/https://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Events.aspx?x=mtgevent/gpvan08/welcome).
