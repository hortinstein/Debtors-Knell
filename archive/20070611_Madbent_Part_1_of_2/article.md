If we took out the [Dark Confidant](https://scryfall.com/card/fin/94/dark-confidant)s entirely, this would put the entire deck (including sideboard) into budget range. There is a similar problem here to the one I found when adapting the Rosewater Black deck. Taking out [Dark Confidant](https://scryfall.com/card/fin/94/dark-confidant) / [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) neuters one of the greatest strengths of both of these decks—card advantage.

Dark Confidant and Phyrexian ArenaBoth [Dark Confidant](https://scryfall.com/card/fin/94/dark-confidant) and [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) are one-sided [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine)s. Everyone loves [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine)—except that [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine) is strictly card disadvantage. It's all simple math. When you play a [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine), you are down a card (the [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine) itself). Playing a [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine) in-and-of itself does *nothing*—you are putting out an artifact that will have no effect on your position until the following turn! This gets worse when your opponent draws for their turn. Suddenly, they are up a card from where they would have been otherwise. Even if they destroy the [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine) at that point, you are still down a card.

Best Case Scenario:
 Your Turn: You play [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine). (-1 Card Advantage)
 Your Opponent's Turn: They draw their regular card, plus the [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine) card (-2 Card Advantage)
 Your Turn: You draw your regular card, plus a [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine) card (-1 Card Advantage)

Worst Case Scenario:
 Your Turn: You play [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine). (-1 Card Advantage)
 Your Opponent's Turn: They draw their regular card, plus the [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine) card (-2 Card Advantage). They then use a [Naturalize](https://scryfall.com/card/m19/190/naturalize) to kill your [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine) (-1 Card Advantage—their extra card drawn takes out your [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine)).

In the worst-case scenario, that [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine) could have been any other card, and it would have given you more benefit – a land, a sorcery, anything. The net result, as a [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine), is that you have played a card that benefited only your opponent (they drew an extra card first) and that extra card killed your [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine), leaving them where they would have otherwise have been (one card drawn that turn) and you down a card (the [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine) that did nothing).

[Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) and [Dark Confidant](https://scryfall.com/card/fin/94/dark-confidant) are so powerful because they break this symmetry. Both only benefit their controller (in this case you), so there is no risk of card disadvantage by playing them.

Best Case Scenario:
 Your Turn: You play [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) (-1 Card Advantage)
 Your Next Turn: You draw your regular card, plus your [Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/tsb/117/arena) card (+0 Card Advantage)
 Your Next Turn: You draw your regular card, plus your [Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/tsb/117/arena) card (+1 Card Advantage)
 Your Next Turn: You draw your regular card, plus your [Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/tsb/117/arena) card (+2 Card Advantage)

In this case, you break even on the [Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/tsb/117/arena) the first turn after you play it, since the extra card you draw replaces the [Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/tsb/117/arena) itself. This is similar for [Dark Confidant](https://scryfall.com/card/fin/94/dark-confidant), except that [Dark Confidant](https://scryfall.com/card/fin/94/dark-confidant) can also swing for 2 damage.

Worst Case Scenario:
 Your Turn: You play [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) (-1 Card Advantage)
 Your Opponent's Turn: They [Disenchant](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/572/disenchant) [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) (+0 Card Advantage)

In the worst-case scenario, your opponent has traded one card ([Disenchant](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/572/disenchant)) for one card ([Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena)) leaving this as a null-sum equation. This is different than the [Howling Mine](https://scryfall.com/card/c16/257/howling-mine) example, because in that case you *gave* them an extra card in the process of having your card-drawing engine killed. In the [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) example, the card you drew (one card: [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena)) was killed by the one card they drew (one card: [Disenchant](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/572/disenchant)).

[![Garza's Assassin](https://api.scryfall.com/cards/csp/57?format=image&version=normal)](https://scryfall.com/card/csp/57/garzas-assassin)

While the Rosewater Black deck tries to overwhelm the opponent by sheer volume of quick threats, the Sato discard deck wins through card advantage. [Garza's Assassin](https://scryfall.com/card/csp/57/garzas-assassin) can be brought back multiple times, so it is one card that can deal with many. [Call to the Netherworld](https://scryfall.com/card/c19/108/call-to-the-netherworld) helps turn [Smallpox](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/139/smallpox) into a winning proposition. If you have a board of only lands, you can kill an opponent's creature, land, and card in hand in exchange for a land, and [Call to the Netherworld](https://scryfall.com/card/c19/108/call-to-the-netherworld) bringing back a previously-killed creature (three for two advantage—you end up with a creature in your hand, and a [Smallpox](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/139/smallpox) and land in your graveyard, with your opponent ending up with a land, creature and card-from-hand in the graveyard. Your [Call to the Netherworld](https://scryfall.com/card/c19/108/call-to-the-netherworld) is a wash with the creature that is now in your hand).

It's the interaction of [Call to the Netherworld](https://scryfall.com/card/c19/108/call-to-the-netherworld) with self-discard effects that most piques my interest about this deck. One huge problem I found with the black-red madness deck was that of inconsistent mana draws. The deck has double-black and double-red mana costs all over the place, including several color-specific activation costs on permanents ([Phyrexian Totem](https://scryfall.com/card/dde/20/phyrexian-totem), Jaya Ballard), upkeep costs ([Stingscourger](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/192/stingscourger)) and the practical impossibility of playing [Smallpox](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/139/smallpox) back-to-back with Jaya Ballard and/or Haakon.

The easiest way to fix these sorts of mana issues are with lands. If you have lands that produce several colors of mana, then you will be able to play your spells with more ease. Unfortunately, these sorts of lands are usually rare, and usually cost a pretty penny. Here's the costs I found on black-red lands:

[Blood Crypt](https://scryfall.com/card/rna/245/blood-crypt) is entirely out of the realm of budget pricing—just three of them would total the cost of our entire budget! Likewise, [Graven Cairns](https://scryfall.com/card/dsc/280/graven-cairns) isn't really affordable right now. For the cost of 5 tickets each, I'd rather find a way to shoehorn [Dark Confidant](https://scryfall.com/card/fin/94/dark-confidant) or [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) (4 tickets) into a deck. They would have a much greater impact on winning than fixing just four lands out of the deck.

The good news about the black-red madness build I had during 10 Decks in 10 Weeks is that it is a very low-cost deck as-is. Red Akroma runs for only two tickets right now online, Jaya Ballard is a single ticket, and Haakon costs 2.5 tickets each. Right now the deck costs 20 tickets, and several of the rares (the Akroma, Haakon, and [Null Profusion](https://scryfall.com/card/plc/89/null-profusion)) could be yanked if we wanted to make the deck look more discard-centric. Taking those out in favor of four [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) would leave us at +4.5 tickets (16 for the [Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/tsb/117/arena)s minus 11.5 for the other rares), which is still at a comfortable 24.5 ticket budget.

Naoto Sato Discard

 Weaknesses: Not budget. Discard is useless once opponent gets into topdeck mode—though the presence of [The Rack](https://scryfall.com/card/tsb/113/the-rack) helps mitigate this. Can't deal with non-creature permanents.

Back to the Rak

 Weaknesses: [Bottled Cloister](https://scryfall.com/card/rvr/252/bottled-cloister) has a much bigger drawback than [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) or [Dark Confidant](https://scryfall.com/card/fin/94/dark-confidant)—if it dies, you lose your hand! Can't deal with non-creature permanents.

Let's face it—black and red decks can't easily deal with enchantments. There aren't a lot of great artifact-kill spells that you'd want to maindeck in those colors. There could be a space for land destruction if warranted ([Stone Rain](https://scryfall.com/card/9ed/221/stone-rain), [Demolish](https://scryfall.com/card/rvr/106/demolish) and [Wrecking Ball](https://scryfall.com/card/cma/198/wrecking-ball) spring to mind), but four of these five decks have something in common—they are looking to be the offensive deck. Yes, black-red madness was a defensive deck, but the other four come out of the gates swinging, and hope to keep the opponent on the ropes the entire time.

[![Gathan Raiders](https://api.scryfall.com/cards/tsr/165?format=image&version=normal)](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/165/gathan-raiders)

*Future Sight* added quite a few interesting madness and hellbent cards to the mix. On the madness end, you get Grave Scrabbler and Ichor Slick. On the hellbent end, you get Cutthroat *il*-Dal, [Gathan Raiders](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/165/gathan-raiders), and [Keldon Megaliths](https://scryfall.com/card/jvc/58/keldon-megaliths). You also get Gibbering Descent, which has *both* madness and hellbent! Of these cards, two have already started seeing tournament play in both MSS events and Regionals testing—[Gathan Raiders](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/165/gathan-raiders) and [Keldon Megaliths](https://scryfall.com/card/jvc/58/keldon-megaliths).

If we're to take the strengths and weaknesses of these five decks and combine them into one post-*Future Sight* concoction, we need to examine what we want out of the deck. This begs the following questions:

1. Does this deck want to be offensive or defensive?
2. Does this deck want to be mono-black, mono-red or black-red?
3. What kill condition / mechanics do we want to focus on using?
4. Assuming we want to maximize our budget, where are our tickets best spent?

Question #1: Does this deck want to be offensive or defensive?

Answer: Offensive. As stated, four of the five decks did well in testing, and all four of those decks were the offensive black or black-red decks. Black and red decks can be built that play the long game, but we're looking to keep things short and sweet. Both hellbent and madness reward this style of play—hellbent by encouraging playing all of your cards out of hand as fast as possible, and madness by encouraging you to discard cards out of your hand on purpose.

Question #2: Does this deck want to be mono-black, mono-red, or black-red?

Answer: I believe we want this to be a black-red build. I'm satisfied with Little Big Black as a mono-black weenie deck, and the Sato deck can easily be adjusted to lose Confidants in favor of either more discard, [Bottled Cloister](https://scryfall.com/card/rvr/252/bottled-cloister)s, or some other budget card-drawing solution to make it instantly fall into budget range. Black and red have a lot of synergy, and so I want to take advantage of the colors together.

Question #3: What kill condition / mechanics do we want to focus on using?

Answer: The sky is the limit on this one. The two most obvious mechanics are hellbent and madness, and the two work together. If you want your deck to focus on discarding cards on purpose, this can both enable madness, and empty your hand quickly for hellbent. The two are not mutually exclusive—madness happens on the way to becoming hellbent.

Question #4: Assuming we want to maximize our budget, where are our tickets best spent?

Answer: Ah, now that's the million dollar question! With all the problems with budgeting in dual lands, [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) and [Dark Confidant](https://scryfall.com/card/fin/94/dark-confidant), can we make a black-red deck that doesn't run into major budgetary constraints that combines both madness and hellbent? Do we even want something like [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena) in a deck that also contains hellbent cards?

The answer to the last question is "Yes." If the deck contains multiple discard outlets, it doesn't matter if we're drawing multiple cards a turn—we will find a way to get rid of those cards if we need to enable hellbent. So for the first time, let's start this sucker up with four copies of [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena). Feel the power! There go 16 tickets of our 30 ticket budget!

In: 4 [Phyrexian Arena](https://scryfall.com/card/fdn/180/phyrexian-arena)

Now there are a number of good madness cards out there right now, and the best of them in Standard are [Fiery Temper](https://scryfall.com/card/inr/154/fiery-temper) (4 for a ticket), [Reckless Wurm](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/181/reckless-wurm) (4 for a ticket), and [Call to the Netherworld](https://scryfall.com/card/c19/108/call-to-the-netherworld). Let's add in copies of each of these to the deck.

In: 4 [Fiery Temper](https://scryfall.com/card/inr/154/fiery-temper), 3 [Reckless Wurm](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/181/reckless-wurm), 3 [Call to the Netherworld](https://scryfall.com/card/c19/108/call-to-the-netherworld)

In order to make sure that mana isn't too big of an issue for this deck, we're going to focus on the majority of the deck being Black, with Red as the splash. If all goes according to plan, both [Fiery Temper](https://scryfall.com/card/inr/154/fiery-temper) and [Reckless Wurm](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/181/reckless-wurm) will only cost a single red to play (madness), so they are an easy splash.

[Gathan Raiders](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/165/gathan-raiders) have been making a splash as well—they are a great madness enabler, plus they can quickly pump from 3/3 to 5/5 in the mid-game. With madness to mitigate their morph / discard effect, they are a 3/3 for three that can have an advantage! As a morph, they might as well be colorless—I can play them regardless of which lands I have in play.

In: 4 [Gathan Raiders](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/165/gathan-raiders)

This deck will have a powerful punch to throw out there starting on turn three, but it won't be a great beatdown before then. Because of this, [Smallpox](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/139/smallpox) is still an ideal choice for the deck—it can kill early creatures, enable madness and hellbent, and garner card advantage through madness. [Phyrexian Totem](https://scryfall.com/card/dde/20/phyrexian-totem) also works well with hellbent and [Smallpox](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/139/smallpox)—it can dodge this particular removal spell, and it is a threat that you can play to the board early and enable later.

In: 3 [Phyrexian Totem](https://scryfall.com/card/dde/20/phyrexian-totem), 3 [Smallpox](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/139/smallpox)

[![Deepcavern Imp](https://api.scryfall.com/cards/tsr/110?format=image&version=normal)](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/110/deepcavern-imp)

There's another creature along the lines of [Gathan Raiders](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/165/gathan-raiders) that saw print in *Future Sight*: [Deepcavern Imp](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/110/deepcavern-imp)! This is the black version of [Skyknight Legionnaire](https://scryfall.com/card/rvr/225/skyknight-legionnaire), which was a fine, playable card. As with the Raiders, the drawback of "discard a card" can easily be turned into card advantage with madness and hellbent.

In: 4 [Deepcavern Imp](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/110/deepcavern-imp)

As additional early offense, [Mindlash Sliver](https://scryfall.com/card/tsp/118/mindlash-sliver) is a fine choice for the deck. It can cause discard at instant-speed, plus it helps to enable hellbent and madness. [Rakdos Guildmage](https://scryfall.com/card/rvr/214/rakdos-guildmage) acts as both creature removal and discard, so it has a place in the deck as well. It won't usually enable madness, but it will help with hellbent.

In: 4 [Mindlash Sliver](https://scryfall.com/card/tsp/118/mindlash-sliver), 4 [Rakdos Guildmage](https://scryfall.com/card/rvr/214/rakdos-guildmage)

This brings us up to 36 cards and 20 tickets in budget. As for lands, we can easily shoehorn four copies of [Tresserhorn Sinks](https://scryfall.com/card/csp/150/tresserhorn-sinks) into this deck—we don't need mana usually until turn two, so the comes-into-play tapped effect on the Sinks isn't too bad.

One of the best reasons to play for hellbent is [Keldon Megaliths](https://scryfall.com/card/jvc/58/keldon-megaliths), the newest Prodigal Sorcerer variant—except as a land! Three Megaliths seems about right for the deck, since we are more black-than-red heavy, and the Megaliths are another land that comes into play tapped.

[![Keldon Megaliths](https://api.scryfall.com/cards/jvc/58?format=image&version=normal)](https://scryfall.com/card/jvc/58/keldon-megaliths)

As for the rest of the deck, I added ten [Swamp](https://scryfall.com/card/msh/291/swamp)s and seven [Mountain](https://scryfall.com/card/msc/858/mountain)s as the initial mana base.

In: 3 [Keldon Megaliths](https://scryfall.com/card/jvc/58/keldon-megaliths), 10 [Swamp](https://scryfall.com/card/msh/291/swamp), 7 [Mountain](https://scryfall.com/card/msc/858/mountain)

This leaves us at 60 cards main-deck, and a budget of 21.5 tickets. Since this is going to be tested in the tournament practice room, we still need to build a 15-card sideboard.

[Blood Moon](https://scryfall.com/card/2xm/118/blood-moon) still only costs 1 ticket each, and it is a great hoser in this dual-land filled world of color-fixing. They are a four-of for this deck. Weenie-hoser favorite [Pyroclasm](https://scryfall.com/card/dsk/149/pyroclasm) is an additional four-of, for a total of six tickets in sideboard budget with seven cards left to go:

Sideboard in: 4 [Blood Moon](https://scryfall.com/card/2xm/118/blood-moon), 4 [Pyroclasm](https://scryfall.com/card/dsk/149/pyroclasm)

Dealing with the graveyard is important, especially since a lot of people are going to be running dredge decks that are centered around [Bridge from Below](https://scryfall.com/card/uma/87/bridge-from-below). [Yixlid Jailer](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/149/yixlid-jailer) (0.5 tickets each) will hose these decks, leaving us at 29.5 tickets, with three sideboard slots left. For the last three slots, I'll add in triple [Shadow Guildmage](https://scryfall.com/card/tsb/46/shadow-guildmage), to help deal with weenie decks (these are commons out of Mirage).

Sideboard In: 4 [Yixlid Jailer](https://scryfall.com/card/tsr/149/yixlid-jailer), 3 [Shadow Guildmage](https://scryfall.com/card/tsb/46/shadow-guildmage)
